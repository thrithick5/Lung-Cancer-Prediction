What is Lung Cancer?

Lung cancer is a type of cancer that begins in the lungs. Your lungs are two spongy organs in your chest that take in oxygen when you inhale and release carbon dioxide when you exhale.

It is the leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide. People who smoke have the greatest risk of lung cancer, though lung cancer can also occur in people who have never smoked. The risk of lung cancer increases with the length of time and number of cigarettes you've smoked.

There are two main types of lung cancer:

  • Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC): Occurs almost exclusively in heavy smokers and is less common.
  • Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC): An umbrella term for several types of lung cancers that behave in a similar way, such as squamous cell carcinoma, adenocarcinoma, and large cell carcinoma.

Key Risk Factors

Smoking

The number one risk factor. Includes cigarettes, cigars, and pipes. The earlier you quit, the better.

Secondhand Smoke

Inhaling smoke from others increases your risk, even if you don't smoke.

Radon Gas

A natural radioactive gas that can build up in homes and is a leading cause for non-smokers.

Asbestos

Workplace exposure to asbestos and other carcinogens can significantly increase risk.

Family History

Having a parent, sibling, or child with lung cancer can increase your risk.

Air Pollution

Long-term exposure to high levels of air pollution may slightly increase the risk.

Common Symptoms

Lung cancer symptoms may not be apparent in early stages. See a doctor if you experience any of these:

Persistent Cough

Chest Pain

Shortness of Breath

Hoarseness

Unexplained Weight Loss

Bone Pain

Coughing up Blood

Headache

Screening Process

Screening is looking for cancer before a person has any symptoms. This can help find cancer at an early stage when it may be easier to treat.

The recommended screening test for lung cancer is low-dose computed tomography (LDCT). An LDCT scan takes detailed pictures of your lungs, which can show abnormalities that might be cancer.

Who should be screened?

Screening is generally recommended for adults aged 50 to 80 years who:

  • Have a 20 pack-year or more smoking history, and
  • Smoke now or have quit within the past 15 years.

Consult with your healthcare provider to see if lung cancer screening is right for you.

Prevention

While you can't prevent every case, you can significantly reduce your risk. Here are the most effective steps you can take.

Don't Smoke

The single most important prevention step. If you smoke, quit. If you don't, never start.

Test for Radon

Use a home test kit to check for radon levels, especially in your basement.

Eat a Healthy Diet

A diet rich in fruits and vegetables may help reduce your risk of lung cancer.
